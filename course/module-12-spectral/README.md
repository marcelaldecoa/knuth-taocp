In 1968 George Marsaglia published a paper with the unimprovable title
*"Random numbers fall mainly in the planes."* Every linear congruential
generator — no matter how carefully tuned — produces points in t dimensions
that lie on a family of parallel hyperplanes. The only question is *how many*
planes, and the **spectral test** answers it exactly. It is, in Knuth's
words, by far the most powerful test known for linear congruential
generators: the bad ones it condemns include IBM's infamous RANDU, which
silently corrupted a decade of published simulations. By the end of this
module you will compute the test *exactly* — integer arithmetic, no
approximations — and issue the same verdicts Knuth's Table 1 does.

> *see* it: the Museum's [Loom of Chaos](https://marcelaldecoa.github.io/knuth-taocp/museum/exhibit-2.1-loom-of-chaos.html)
> plots an LCG's pairs (Xₙ, Xₙ₊₁) so the lattice of parallel lines is visible to
> the eye — few coarse stripes for a bad multiplier, dense white noise for a
> good one. Load its RANDU preset to meet the generator this module condemns.

## 1. Random numbers fall mainly in the planes

A linear congruential generator (LCG) produces

    x_{n+1} = (a·x_n + c) mod m.

To study how random the sequence *looks in t dimensions*, form the
**overlapping t-tuples**

    P_n = (x_n, x_{n+1}, ..., x_{n+t-1}) ∈ [0, m)^t.

Overlapping, because that is what consumers experience: a simulation that
draws three coordinates in a row gets exactly these triples, and the serial
tests of §3.3.2 measure exactly this point set. A truly random source would
scatter the P_n uniformly through the cube. An LCG cannot: the whole point
set has a rigid crystalline structure, and we are about to compute its
worst defect.

### The increment is irrelevant

We will work with c = 0 throughout, and here is why nothing is lost. Run
the same multiplier with increment c from the same x_0 and compare with the
c = 0 sequence: by induction, the term with increment is

    x_n(c) = a^n·x_0 + (a^{n-1} + ... + a + 1)·c   (mod m),

so the tuple starting at position n is the c = 0 tuple **plus the fixed
vector** (0, c, (a+1)c, (a²+a+1)c, ...) mod m — the same shift for every n.
The entire point set is *translated*; translations change neither spacings
nor plane counts, so every quantity in this module is independent of c.
(This is Knuth's observation at the start of §3.3.4: the spectral test
depends only on a and m.)

## 2. The lattice theorem

A **lattice** in Z^t is the set of all integer combinations of some basis
vectors. The structure theorem for LCG tuples is almost embarrassingly
short:

**Theorem (lattice structure).** Every overlapping t-tuple of the sequence
x_{n+1} = a·x_n mod m lies in the lattice L generated by

    v = (1, a, a², ..., a^{t-1})   and   m·e_2, m·e_3, ..., m·e_t.

*Proof (two lines).* Coordinate i of the tuple starting at x_n is
x_n·a^{i-1} mod m. So the tuple equals x_n·v minus a suitable multiple of m
in each coordinate after the first (the first needs none: x_n is already in
[0, m)) — and those corrections are exactly what m·e_2, ..., m·e_t supply. ∎

Note that m·e_1 is also in L: m·v − a·(m·e_2) − a²·(m·e_3) − ... = (m, 0,
..., 0). So L contains all of m·Z^t plus the m distinct multiples of v mod
m; its determinant (volume of a fundamental cell) is m^t / m = m^{t-1}.
The cube [0, m)^t has volume m^t and the sequence visits at most m points
of it — one point per cell, a perfect crystal.

*Honest caveat:* the theorem says the tuples lie **in** L ∩ [0, m)^t; over
a full period they typically fill it (that is Knuth's setting), while a
short run or a restricted cycle (e.g. odd seeds for m = 2^31) fills a
subset. The *defects* we compute are properties of L, hence upper bounds on
the quality of any run.

## 3. Dual vectors, families of planes, and ν_t

Call u = (u_1, ..., u_t) ∈ Z^t a **dual vector** (u ≠ 0) if

    u_1 + a·u_2 + a²·u_3 + ... + a^{t-1}·u_t ≡ 0  (mod m).

**Why "dual":** u·v ≡ 0 (mod m) by definition, and u·(m·e_i) ≡ 0 trivially,
so u·w ≡ 0 (mod m) for *every* w ∈ L. In particular, again in two lines:
every tuple P_n satisfies

    u·P_n ≡ x_n·(u_1 + a·u_2 + ... + a^{t-1}·u_t) ≡ 0  (mod m),

because coordinate i of P_n is congruent to x_n·a^{i-1}. So the entire
point set lies on the family of parallel hyperplanes

    u·x = k·m,     k ∈ Z.

**How many planes meet the cube?** For x ∈ [0, m)^t, split the dual vector
into its positive and negative parts: P = Σ_{u_i>0} u_i and N = Σ_{u_i<0}
|u_i|. Then

    −(m−1)·N  ≤  u·x  ≤  (m−1)·P,

so k = (u·x)/m ranges over the integers of [−(m−1)N/m, (m−1)P/m]; counting
them gives exactly **N + P − 1 planes** when u has entries of both signs
(and P when it has one sign, where the k = 0 plane touches only the
origin). Either way, the family has at most

    |u_1| + |u_2| + ... + |u_t|   planes.

Small dual vector ⇒ few planes ⇒ bad generator.

**How far apart are the planes?** Adjacent hyperplanes u·x = km and
u·x = (k+1)m are at distance m/‖u‖ (the standard point–plane distance).
Short dual vector ⇒ wide empty slabs.

Both defects are governed by the same quantity, and that is the test:

**Definition (spectral test).** For t ≥ 2,

    ν_t² = min { u_1² + ... + u_t² :
                 u_1 + a·u_2 + ... + a^{t-1}·u_t ≡ 0 (mod m),  u ≠ 0 }.

ν_t is the length of the shortest nonzero dual vector. The maximum gap
between adjacent covering planes, over all families, is m/ν_t; and the
family cut out by that shortest u has at most Σ|u_i| ≤ √t·ν_t planes
(Cauchy–Schwarz). Large ν_t is good: every direction's dual vectors are
then long, so no direction sees wide empty slabs or few planes.

### The RANDU catastrophe, exactly

RANDU is a = 65539 = 2^16 + 3, m = 2^31, c = 0 (odd seeds). The multiplier
was chosen so that a·x could be computed with two shifts and two adds. But

    (a − 3)² = 2^32 ≡ 0  (mod 2^31)   ⟹   a² ≡ 6a − 9  (mod 2^31),

so **u = (9, −6, 1) is a dual vector**: 9 − 6a + a² ≡ 0 (mod 2^31). Every
triple the generator will ever produce satisfies

    9·x_n − 6·x_{n+1} + x_{n+2} ≡ 0  (mod 2^31).

Plane count: N = 6, P = 10, so N + P − 1 = **15 planes** (at most
9 + 6 + 1 = 16) carry all 2^29 triples in a cube with 2^31 cells per side.
In stage 3 you will *prove* nothing shorter exists: ν_3² = 9² + 6² + 1² =
118. A good multiplier for m = 2^31 could have achieved ν_3 ≈ 1448 (the
Hermite bound of §6), i.e. thousands of tightly packed plane families
instead of 15 gaping slabs. Marsaglia's title was not hyperbole.

## 4. Two dimensions, exactly: Gauss–Lagrange reduction

For t = 2 the dual lattice is L* = {(u_1, u_2) : u_1 + a·u_2 ≡ 0 (mod m)}.
It has an obvious basis:

**Lemma.** L* is generated by v_1 = (m, 0) and v_2 = (−a, 1).

*Proof.* Both satisfy the congruence. Conversely if (u_1, u_2) ∈ L*, then
u_1 = −a·u_2 + km for some integer k, so (u_1, u_2) = u_2·v_2 + k·v_1. ∎

ν_2² is the squared length of the shortest nonzero vector of L*, and in
rank 2 there is an exact, fast, ancient algorithm for that — Lagrange's
reduction, usually credited to Gauss. It is Euclid's algorithm acting on
vectors: repeatedly subtract the best integer multiple of the shorter
vector from the longer one.

### Algorithm G (Gauss–Lagrange reduction)

Given a basis v_1, v_2 of a rank-2 lattice, find a shortest nonzero vector.

```text
G1. [Initialize.] v1 <- (m, 0), v2 <- (-a, 1).
G2. [Order.]      If |v1| < |v2|, interchange v1 and v2.
G3. [Reduce.]     Let q be the nearest integer to (v1·v2)/(v2·v2);
                  set v1 <- v1 - q·v2.
G4. [Done?]       If |v1| >= |v2|, terminate; v2 is a shortest nonzero
                  vector and ν_2² = v2·v2. Otherwise return to G2.
```

Hand-trace it on the module's toy generator a = 137, m = 256 (do this on
paper — it is the module's Fibonacci-trace moment):

| pass | v1 | v2 | ‖v1‖² | ‖v2‖² | v1·v2 | q | new v1 | new ‖v1‖² |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | (256, 0) | (−137, 1) | 65536 | 18770 | −35072 | −2 | (−18, 2) | 328 |
| 2 | (−137, 1) | (−18, 2) | 18770 | 328 | 2468 | 8 | (7, −15) | 274 |
| 3 | (−18, 2) | (7, −15) | 328 | 274 | −156 | −1 | (−11, −13) | 290 ≥ 274: **stop** |

Answer: shortest vector (7, −15), so **ν_2² = 274**. Sanity: is (7, −15)
dual? The congruence for (−7, 15): −7 + 137·15 = 2048 = 8·256 ✓ (a lattice
contains v iff it contains −v). The pair point set of this generator lies
on |−7| + |15| − 1 = 21 lines, spaced 256/√274 ≈ 15.5 apart.

**Termination.** Steps G2–G3 leave the lattice unchanged (swaps and shears
are unimodular). If G4 does not stop, the new v_1 is strictly shorter than
v_2, which was the *shorter* of the previous pair — so min(‖v_1‖², ‖v_2‖²),
a positive integer, strictly decreases on every continuing pass. A
decreasing sequence of positive integers is finite. (And if q = 0 the
vector is unchanged, but then ‖v_1‖ ≥ ‖v_2‖ from G2, so G4 stops: no
infinite idling.) This is module 01's proof pattern — invariant plus a
decreasing quantity — operating on norms instead of remainders.

**Optimality.** At termination, two facts hold: ‖v_1‖ ≥ ‖v_2‖, and — the
property of rounding to the *nearest* integer — |v_1·v_2| ≤ ‖v_2‖²/2. Now
take any nonzero lattice vector w = α·v_1 + β·v_2 (α, β ∈ Z).

- If β = 0: ‖w‖ = |α|·‖v_1‖ ≥ ‖v_2‖.
- If α = 0: ‖w‖ = |β|·‖v_2‖ ≥ ‖v_2‖.
- Otherwise:
  ‖w‖² = α²‖v_1‖² + 2αβ(v_1·v_2) + β²‖v_2‖²
       ≥ α²‖v_2‖² − 2|α||β|·(‖v_2‖²/2) + β²‖v_2‖²
       = (α² − |α||β| + β²)·‖v_2‖² ≥ ‖v_2‖²,

because α² − |α||β| + β² = ((|α| − |β|)² + α² + β²)/2 ≥ 1 for nonzero
integers. So v_2 is genuinely shortest. ∎ Note where integrality did the
work: for real α = β = ½ the quadratic form dips to ¼. The final basis is
called *reduced*; this is the 2-dimensional case of what LLL approximates
in higher rank.

## 5. Three dimensions: a certified search

For t = 3 the dual condition is u_1 + a·u_2 + a²·u_3 ≡ 0 (mod m). Choose
(u_2, u_3) freely; then u_1 is pinned down *modulo m*, and the best
representative is obvious:

**Lemma (centered residue).** Fix (u_2, u_3) ≠ (0, 0) and let r ∈ [0, m) be
the residue of a·u_2 + a²·u_3. Among all valid u_1 ≡ −r (mod m), the one of
smallest absolute value is the centered residue — u_1 = −r if 2r ≤ m, else
u_1 = m − r — and it satisfies |u_1| ≤ m/2. Any other member of the class
differs by a nonzero multiple of m and is at least as far from 0. ∎

So the 3-D minimum is a search over the (u_2, u_3) plane. The trouble: the
plane is infinite. The fix: a bound that *certifies itself*.

### Algorithm C (certified bounded search)

```text
C1. [Initialize.] best <- m²  (the class (u2,u3) = (0,0) contributes
                  (m, 0, 0); u = 0 itself is excluded).  B <- 1.
C2. [Scan.]       For all (u2, u3) in [-B, B]² except (0,0): set u1 to the
                  centered residue of -(a·u2 + a²·u3) mod m, and
                  best <- min(best, u1² + u2² + u3²).
C3. [Certified?]  If B² >= best, terminate: ν_3² = best.
C4. [Grow.]       B <- 2B; return to C2.
```

**Why termination at C3 is a proof, not a hope.** Suppose B² ≥ best and
some dual vector u is shorter than best. Where is it? If |u_2| > B or
|u_3| > B, then ‖u‖² ≥ u_2² + u_3² > B² ≥ best — contradiction. So
(u_2, u_3) lies in the scanned square; but there we already took the best
possible u_1 for its class (the centered-residue lemma), so u cannot beat
best either. Hence best = ν_3² exactly. The search bound is justified *by
the answer it found* — the same trick as branch-and-bound, in its simplest
possible clothing.

**Why C4 terminates.** Hermite's theorem (next section) gives
ν_3² ≤ 2^{1/3}·m^{2/3} < 2.2×10^6 for m ≤ 2^31, so once B = 4096 we have
B² > ν_3² ≥ best-after-scan and C3 fires. Total work: the doubling scans
sum to at most (4/3)·(2·4096+1)² ≈ 9×10^7 cheap iterations — the geometric
series is dominated by its last term, a bound you proved for table doubling
in earlier modules. (In practice RANDU certifies at B = 16 and the minimal
standard at B = 1024.)

Why not extend Gauss reduction instead? Rank-3 reduction (or LLL) gives a
*nearly* shortest vector with more delicate proofs; Knuth's Algorithm S
does exact enumeration with Cholesky-style pruning. Algorithm C is the
honest kernel of the latter: exact, certified, and slow only in ways we can
afford at t = 3.

## 6. Figures of merit: how good could ν_t possibly be?

Raw ν_t values are incomparable across moduli — ν_3 = 639 is meaningless
until you know how large ν_3 *could* be for that m. Two normalizations:

**Hermite's constants.** For any rank-t lattice of determinant d, the
shortest nonzero vector satisfies λ_1² ≤ γ_t·d^{2/t}, where

    γ_2 = 2/√3 ≈ 1.1547   (hexagonal lattice, Lagrange/Gauss),
    γ_3 = 2^{1/3} ≈ 1.2599 (face-centered cubic packing, Gauss).

Our dual lattice L* = {u : Σ u_i a^{i-1} ≡ 0 (mod m)} has index m in Z^t
(one congruence mod m), hence determinant m. Therefore

    ν_2² ≤ (2/√3)·m        and        ν_3² ≤ 2^{1/3}·m^{2/3}.

These bounds are load-bearing twice in the lab: they justify the
brute-force cutoff in stage 2 (|u_2| ≤ √(γ_2·m) suffices) and the
termination of Algorithm C in stage 3.

**Knuth's μ_t** (§3.3.4, Eq. (37)) compares ν_t to the ideal by volume:

    μ_t = (volume of the t-ball of radius ν_t) / m
        = π^{t/2}·ν_t^t / ((t/2)!·m),

so

    μ_2 = π·ν_2²/m          and          μ_3 = (4/3)·π·ν_3³/m

(the half-integer factorial: (3/2)! = (3/4)√π). Interpretation: the dual
lattice has one point per volume m, so μ_t is the expected number of
lattice points in a ball whose radius is the *actual* shortest vector — if
μ_t is tiny, that vector is anomalously short and the generator is bad in
t dimensions. Hermite caps the scale: μ_2 ≤ 2π/√3 ≈ 3.63 and
μ_3 ≤ (4/3)π√2 ≈ 5.92. **Knuth's rule of thumb: μ_t ≥ 0.1 passes, μ_t ≥ 1
is excellent.**

The verdicts you will compute in the lab (compare Knuth's Table 1):

| generator | m | ν_2² | ν_3² | μ_2 | μ_3 | verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| toy, a = 137 | 256 | 274 | 30 | 3.36 | 2.69 | great *for its size* |
| RANDU, a = 65539 | 2^31 | 2 147 221 514 | **118** | 3.14 | **2.5×10⁻⁶** | catastrophic in 3-D |
| minimal standard, a = 16807 | 2^31−1 | 16807²+1 | 408 197 | 0.41 | 0.51 | passes; unspectacular |
| a = 48271 (`std::minstd_rand`) | 2^31−1 | 1 990 735 345 | 1 433 881 | 2.91 | 3.35 | excellent for this m |

Two morals. First, the test is *per dimension*: RANDU's pairs are nearly
perfect (μ_2 ≈ π, close to the Hermite ceiling!) while its triples are
ruinous — you must test every t your application consumes. Second,
multipliers are not commodities: 16807
and 48271 cost the same to evaluate, and one is an order of magnitude
better. That is why the revision happened.

### Scope note (honesty about t ≤ 3)

Knuth's Algorithm S carries this program to t = 4, 5, ..., 8: it maintains
dual and primal bases simultaneously, transforms them as t grows, and
enumerates candidates inside an ellipsoid with Cholesky-factorization
bounds instead of our square. Every idea you implement here — dual lattice,
exact integer arithmetic, a search bound certified by the best value found
— reappears there, industrial-strength. We stop at t = 3 because rank-2
reduction and one certified 2-parameter search teach the entire method
without the bookkeeping; if you want the rest, exercise W6 and §3.3.4 are
waiting.

## 7. Why it's done this way

- **Exact integer arithmetic, `i128`.** ν_t² is a *minimum over a lattice*;
  a single rounding error can report a vector that does not exist or miss
  one that does, and a "certification" computed in floating point certifies
  nothing. Norms reach m² ≈ 2^62; `i128` holds them with room to spare.
  Floating point enters exactly once, in μ_t, *after* the integer minimum
  is frozen.
- **The dual lattice, not the primal.** The failure mode users care about
  is planes-and-slabs, and plane families correspond one-to-one to dual
  vectors, with gap m/‖u‖. Minimizing over the dual *is* maximizing the
  worst slab — and the dual basis is available by inspection, no inversion
  needed.
- **Reduction in 2-D, enumeration in 3-D.** Rank 2 is the one case where a
  greedy reduction provably finds the optimum (§4's proof), so use it.
  At rank 3 greedy is no longer provably optimal, but enumeration with a
  self-certifying bound is still cheap — so we buy the proof with cycles.
  This mirrors real practice: LLL/BKZ reduce, then an exact enumeration
  finishes.
- **c = 0 without loss.** The increment translates the point set (§1);
  carrying it through every formula would buy nothing and obscure the
  lattice. Knuth drops it in the first paragraph of §3.3.4 for the same
  reason.
- **Overlapping tuples.** Non-overlapping tuples of a full-period LCG form
  essentially the same lattice (a sub-sequence of an LCG is an LCG with
  multiplier a^t), but overlapping tuples are what serial correlation
  actually sees, and they make the lattice theorem a one-liner.

## 8. Stage-by-stage lab guide

Open `labs/module-12-spectral/src/lab.rs`. Run `./grade 12`; stages run in
order and stop at the first failure.

### Stage 1 — `tuples` and `is_dual_vector`

Mechanics first. `tuples(a, m, t, x0, count)` generates count + t − 1 terms
of x_{n+1} = a·x_n mod m and windows them; multiply in `i128` before
reducing. `is_dual_vector(u, a, m)` checks Σ u_i·a^{i-1} ≡ 0 (mod m) with
incrementally computed powers (never form a^{t-1} unreduced!) and rejects
u = 0. Coordinates may be negative: `rem_euclid` gives the non-negative
residue Rust's `%` does not. The tests make you verify RANDU's
(9, −6, 1) — and that *every* generated triple obeys the planes equation,
which is the lattice theorem made executable.

### Stage 2 — `nu2_squared`

Algorithm G on the basis (m, 0), (−a, 1), all in `i128`. The one subtlety
is q = *nearest* integer to d/n with n > 0: `(2*d + n).div_euclid(2*n)`
does it (check it against the hand trace: d = −35072, n = 18770 must give
q = −2). The tests cross-check against a brute force whose cutoff is the
Hermite bound — read that helper; its comment is the argument you should be
able to reproduce cold — and pin the hand-traced ν_2²(137, 256) = 274.

### Stage 3 — `nu3_squared_certified`

Algorithm C verbatim. Keep the running residue r = (a·u_2 + a²·u_3) mod m
incremental if you like speed, but correctness only needs the centered
residue chosen right: r vs m − r, smaller absolute value wins. Do not
forget to skip (u_2, u_3) = (0, 0) *inside* the scan while still seeding
best = m². The tests demand RANDU's 118 on the nose, the frozen 408 197 for
the minimal standard, agreement with a full brute force on tiny moduli, and
a witness dual vector achieving your minimum.

### Stage 4 — `mu2` and `mu3`

Ten lines: μ_2 = π·ν_2²/m, μ_3 = (4/3)π·ν_3³/m with ν_3 = √(ν_3²) in f64.
The tests hold you to the reference values to 1e-9 and — the point of the
whole module — to the ordering: 48271 ≻ 16807 ≻ RANDU on μ_3, with five
orders of magnitude between the ends.

## 9. In the real world

- **RANDU's corrupted simulations.** RANDU shipped in IBM's System/360
  Scientific Subroutine Package and was the de facto random source of the
  late 1960s and 1970s. Any Monte Carlo computation that consumed triples —
  particle transport, numerical integration in 3-D, generation of random
  directions — was sampling 15 planes instead of a volume. Results built on
  it had to be re-examined for years afterwards; Knuth's verdict in §3.3.4
  is that its very name deserves contempt, and textbooks still cite it as
  the canonical warning that a generator can pass 1-D tests perfectly while
  being unusable. The deeper lesson: the flaw was *provable in advance* —
  (a−3)² = 2^32 is one line of algebra — but nobody ran the test before
  shipping.
- **Why PCG and xoshiro replaced plain LCGs.** The lattice structure you
  computed is not a bug of bad multipliers; it is a theorem about *all*
  LCGs. Even the best multiplier leaves ν_t ≈ m^{1/t}·(γ_t)^{1/2}, so a
  32-bit LCG is structurally weak in high dimensions, and power-of-two
  moduli additionally make the low k bits cycle with period 2^k. Modern
  designs keep a linear engine but *destroy the lattice at the output*:
  PCG (O'Neill, 2014) advances a 64/128-bit LCG state — with multipliers
  chosen via exactly this spectral theory, and its default multiplier
  6364136223846793005 is the one Knuth ships in MMIX (and the one this
  course's test files use for deterministic data) — then applies a
  state-dependent permutation (xorshift + rotate) so that output tuples no
  longer lie on the state lattice. The xoshiro/xoroshiro family (Blackman &
  Vigna) uses linear recurrences over GF(2) plus a nonlinear scrambler
  (`**` = multiply–rotate–multiply) for the same reason. Both pass
  TestU01's BigCrush, where every raw LCG of comparable size fails —
  largely on lattice-derived tests.
- **Where LCGs are still fine.** As the *state transition* inside PCG and
  in `java.util.Random`-style engines (weak, but understood); as combined
  generators — L'Ecuyer's MRG32k3a, built from two multiple-recursive
  congruential generators chosen by spectral-type criteria, remains a
  workhorse of simulation libraries and GPU stream packages (MATLAB,
  SSJ, cuRAND); and anywhere reproducible, cheap, *statistically
  undemanding* streams are needed: test-data generation (this repo!),
  procedural jitter in graphics, load-balancing salts. The rule: fine when
  nobody consumes k-tuples as geometry, or when an output scrambler stands
  between the lattice and the user. And when someone proposes a new
  multiplier, the spectral test — Fishman–Moore/L'Ecuyer-style exhaustive
  searches, Knuth's Algorithm S — is still exactly how it is vetted.

## 12. Proof techniques you practiced

- **Reduction to a canonical case** — dropping c by exhibiting the point
  sets as translates (§1); one line of algebra deleted a parameter from the
  whole theory.
- **Proof by generators** — the lattice theorem and dual-vector
  annihilation were verified on basis vectors only, and linearity did the
  rest (§2, §3).
- **Counting by bounding a linear form** — the plane count N + P − 1 came
  from trapping u·x in an interval and counting multiples of m inside it
  (§3); the same move produced Lamé-style bounds in module 01.
- **Invariant + strictly decreasing integer quantity** — termination of
  Gauss–Lagrange reduction (§4), the course's oldest pattern, here with
  unimodularity as the invariant and the squared norm as the descent.
- **Optimality via a quadratic-form inequality** — the exchange argument
  α² − |αβ| + β² ≥ 1 turned "locally reduced" into "globally shortest"
  (§4), with integrality load-bearing.
- **Certified enumeration** — a search bound justified *a posteriori* by
  the best value found, plus an a priori Hermite bound to guarantee the
  loop halts (§5, §6). This is the skeleton of every branch-and-bound
  correctness argument you will ever write.

## 13. Where this leads

- **Module 04** gave you the LCG and its period theory (§3.2.1); this
  module is the quality theory that decides *which* full-period generator
  to ship. Together they are Knuth's answer to "what multiplier?"
- The reduction/enumeration pair (Gauss–Lagrange, certified search) is the
  entry point to **lattice algorithms at large**: LLL, BKZ, and the
  shortest-vector problems underlying post-quantum cryptography — where
  §4's optimality argument reappears as the base case of LLL's analysis.
- Knuth's **Algorithm S** (t ≤ 8) adds simultaneous primal/dual basis
  updates and ellipsoid enumeration — a finished branch-and-bound built on
  stage 3's certification idea.
- The statistical companion is §3.3.2's serial test and the empirical
  batteries (TestU01); the spectral test is the *theoretical* member of
  that family — it examines the whole period at once, no sampling.
