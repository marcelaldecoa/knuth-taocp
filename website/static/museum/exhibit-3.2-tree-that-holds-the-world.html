Wing III · The Architecture of Order

The Tree That
Holds the World

B-trees, and keeping order on the disk

VOLUME III — Sorting and Searching SECTION 6.2.4 1970 Bayer & McCreight

The data that runs the world does not fit in memory. A bank's ledger, a search engine's index, the file table of the disk you are reading this from — each is far too large to hold in RAM, so it lives on storage that is vast, cheap, and agonizingly slow. Reaching a random spot on a spinning disk takes about ten milliseconds; reaching a spot in RAM takes about ten nanoseconds. That is a factor of a million. When your data lives on disk, the only number that matters is how many times you have to go and fetch a page — and the whole art is in fetching as few as possible.

A binary search tree, so elegant in memory, is a catastrophe on disk: a billion keys make it thirty levels deep, and following it to a leaf means thirty separate seeks, a third of a second of pure waiting. In 1970 Rudolf Bayer and Ed McCreight, at Boeing's research labs, asked the obvious question nobody had answered: if a disk read hands you a whole page of thousands of bytes for the same cost as one byte, why store a single key per node? Pack hundreds of keys into each node, make the tree short and enormously bushy, and the billion keys collapse into three or four fetches. They called it the B-tree, and it quietly became the most important data structure in the world.

"On disk, depth is destiny. Every level of the tree is another seek, and a seek is an eternity. The B-tree's genius is to be almost no levels deep, no matter how much you pour into it." — after R. Bayer & E. McCreight, 1970

I · Growing from the LeavesHow a B-tree stays balanced

A B-tree of order m obeys one strict discipline: every node holds at most m−1 keys, and every node but the root holds at least half that many, so no page is ever left mostly empty. To insert a key you walk down to the correct leaf and drop it in. If the leaf still fits, you are done. But if the leaf overflows past m−1 keys, it splits: the median key is pushed up into the parent, and the node cleaves into two half-full siblings. That push can overflow the parent, which splits in turn — and if the overflow reaches all the way to the root, the root itself splits and the tree grows one level taller.

This is the B-tree's beautiful trick, and you can watch it below. Ordinary trees grow downward at the leaves; a B-tree grows upward at the root. Because every split is symmetric and the height only ever increases at the very top, all the leaves stay at exactly the same depth, forever. The tree is perfectly balanced by construction — not rebalanced after the fact, but incapable of becoming unbalanced in the first place. Inject records and watch the pages fill, split, and lift their medians skyward.

II · Bushy Beats DeepWhy three reads find a billion keys

The instrument uses a tiny order — three, five, or seven keys per page — so the splits happen often enough to see. Real B-trees are gargantuan by comparison: a database page is typically sixteen kilobytes, holding hundreds or even a thousand keys, so m is measured in the hundreds. The consequence is staggering. The height of a B-tree on N keys is about logmN, and with m ≈ 1000 that logarithm is brutal in the tree's favour: a thousand keys fit in height 2, a million in height 3, a billion in height 4. Four disk reads to find any record among a billion. The same billion in a binary tree would be thirty reads — nearly an order of magnitude more waiting, per lookup, forever.

A binary tree asks one yes-or-no question per expensive disk seek. A B-tree asks hundreds. That is the whole difference, and it is the difference between a database that answers and one that hangs.

Knuth analyses these multiway trees in §6.2.4, and their descendants run essentially all of stored data. The B+ tree — a variant that keeps every record down in the leaves and threads the leaves together in a linked list — is the index behind virtually every relational database: PostgreSQL, MySQL's InnoDB, Oracle, SQL Server. That leaf-thread is what lets a database answer "every order between March and June" by finding the start and then streaming along the leaves in sorted order, without ever climbing back up. The same structure indexes the files on your disk: NTFS, HFS+, APFS, and ext4 all store their directories as B-trees.

III · Order Kept on the DiskThe payoff of the sorting room

This room settles a debt owed by the last one. Merge sort earned its keep, you were told, when the data is too large for RAM and must stream from disk — external sorting. That is exactly how a B-tree is born at scale: to build an index over billions of existing records, you do not insert them one at a time; you external-sort them first, then bulk-load the sorted stream into a B-tree bottom-up, packing each page full. Sorting and searching, the two halves of Volume 3, meet here: the sort puts the world in order, and the tree is what keeps it in order, on the disk, so that any fact among billions is three or four reads away.

"An algorithm must be seen to be believed." — D. E. Knuth

Fill the simulator until it is several levels deep and watch what never happens: it never tilts, never degenerates, never leaves a page half-wasted. Every leaf sits at one depth; every page is at least half full; every lookup costs the height and nothing more. It is one of those rare inventions that is simultaneously simple to state, subtle to prove, and so practically decisive that half a century later we have found nothing better for the job. The tree that holds the world holds it still.
