Wing IV · The Ocean of Possibilities

The Traveling
Salesperson & the Ant

Permutations, combinatorial explosion, and P vs NP

VOLUME IV — Combinatorial Algorithms SECTION 7.2.1.2 1930 · 1972 Menger · Karp

A salesperson must visit a list of cities, each exactly once, and return home, and would like the shortest possible route. That is the entire problem. A child can understand it; a shipping company, a circuit-board driller, and a telescope scheduling its night all live and die by it. And it is, notoriously, one of the hardest problems humanity has ever posed to itself — not because any single tour is hard to measure, but because there are so unspeakably many of them. To be sure you have the shortest, you must, it seems, look at them all. And "all," here, is a number that eats the universe.

Count them. Fix your home city and line up the others in some order — that is a tour. There are (n−1)! such orderings, and since a route and its exact reverse have the same length, the number of genuinely distinct tours is (n−1)!/2. For five cities that is twelve — you could check them on a napkin. For ten cities it is already over a hundred and eighty thousand. For fifteen, it is forty-three billion. For twenty-five, it exceeds the number of grains of sand that would fill the observable universe. The factorial does not grow; it detonates.

"Exponential growth is a dragon that sleeps until you are one city too greedy, and then no computer that will ever be built can wake in time to help you." — the tyranny of the factorial

I · The Lost Time MachineWatch the counter detonate

The instrument below is a machine for feeling this in your body rather than believing it in the abstract. Drop cities on the map, or let it scatter them, and it does two things at once. It counts the tours — the honest (n−1)!/2, as a real number with real digits — and it estimates how long a very fast computer, checking a billion tours every second, would need to examine them all. Press brute force and it begins that examination for real, one tour at a time, drawing each as it goes and climbing the counter. On a handful of cities it finishes in a blink and shows you the provably perfect route. Add a few more and the machine gets lost in time: the counter crawls, the estimate swells past your lifetime, past recorded history, past the age of the universe, and the perfect answer recedes forever out of reach.

Then meet the ant. Ants do not compute optimal tours; they follow a simple rule — go to the nearest place you have not yet been — and the colony's collective path is startlingly good. Press nearest neighbour and a decent route appears instantly, at any number of cities, because the ant's method costs only about n² steps instead of n!. Polish it with 2-opt, untangling crossed edges, and it gets better still. It is almost never perfect — but it arrives before the heat death of the universe, which the salesperson's certainty does not.

Every dot is a city; every line an edge of the current tour. The salesperson wants certainty and must pay the factorial for it; the ant wants only a good route and pays a mere polynomial. The gap between them — a blink versus the age of the universe — is the visceral shape of the question P ≟ NP.

II · The Factorial and the PolynomialTwo kinds of hard

What you are watching is the central drama of theoretical computer science. Some problems are easy in a precise sense: the time to solve them grows as a polynomial in the input size — n, n², n³ — and stays humane as n grows. The ant's nearest-neighbour method is one of these, costing about n². Other problems appear to demand time that grows exponentially or worse — 2ⁿ, n! — and become physically impossible almost immediately. The whole class of "easy" problems is called P. The traveling salesperson, in its exact form, has resisted every attempt to place it there.

Yet notice a strange asymmetry the instrument makes plain: while finding the best tour is agonizing, checking a proposed tour is trivial — add up the distances, done in n steps. Problems whose answers are easy to verify, even if fiendish to find, form the class NP, and TSP is not merely in NP; Richard Karp proved in 1972 that it is NP-complete — as hard as any problem in the class. If anyone ever found a polynomial algorithm for it, every problem in NP would fall at once, and P would equal NP.

Does the ease of checking an answer imply the ease of finding one? That is the question P ≟ NP — worth a million-dollar prize, unsolved for half a century, and quietly present every time you watch the counter detonate.

III · The Wisdom of the AntGood enough, right now

Faced with a dragon it cannot slay, computer science learned to negotiate. If the perfect tour is unreachable, get a provably good one fast: Christofides' algorithm guarantees a route no worse than 1.5× optimal in polynomial time; the Lin–Kernighan heuristic and 2-opt routinely land within a percent or two; Marco Dorigo's ant colony optimization literally simulates pheromone-laying ants to breed excellent tours. And when certainty is truly required, solvers like Concorde combine branch-and-bound with cutting planes to prove optimality on instances of tens of thousands of cities — by being clever enough to avoid looking at almost all of the (n−1)!/2 tours, even though the worst case remains exponential.

"An algorithm must be seen to be believed." — D. E. Knuth

Knuth devotes the fourth volume of his life's work to exactly this ocean — the art of generating and searching through combinatorial possibilities without drowning in them. The traveling salesperson is its emblem: a problem so simple a child grasps it and so hard the universe cannot brute-force it, sitting on the knife-edge between the possible and the impossible that the next two rooms — Knuth's own dancing links, and the logic of satisfiability — are built to walk. Drop one more city on the map and watch certainty slip away. Then ask the ant, and go home before dark.
