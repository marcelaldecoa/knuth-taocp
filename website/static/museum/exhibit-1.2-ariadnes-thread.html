Ariadne's

Pointers, linked lists, and the liberation of memory

VOLUME I — Fundamental Algorithms SECTION 2.2.1–2.2.3 c. 1956 · IPL / LISP

In the myth, Theseus walks into the labyrinth to kill the Minotaur and faces a subtler danger than the monster: the maze itself. Its corridors branch and double back until direction is meaningless. Ariadne's gift is not a map — a map would require seeing the whole maze at once — but a single thread, tied at the door and unspooled step by step. To escape, Theseus needs to know only one thing at each chamber: which way the thread leads next. This is the oldest data structure in Western storytelling, and it is exactly a linked list.

For the first decade of computing, memory was a street of houses with consecutive numbers, and a sequence of data was a row of adjacent houses. To store "the third item" you computed its address; to insert a new item in the middle, you evicted everyone downstream and marched them one house over. Then, around 1956, Allen Newell, Cliff Shaw, and Herbert Simon — building their Information Processing Language — and soon John McCarthy in LISP, made a move so simple it is easy to miss its radicalism: let each datum carry, alongside its value, the address of its successor. The data no longer needs to live in order. It only needs to point.

"Linked memory is the art of letting go of adjacency. Two things are 'next to' each other not because of where they sit, but because one holds the other's address." — after D. E. Knuth, TAOCP Vol. 1, §2.2

I · The Link FieldA datum that knows only what comes next

A node is two things welded together: a data field holding the value, and a link field holding the address of the next node — Knuth writes the empty link, the end of the thread, as Λ. A single distinguished pointer, HEAD, holds the address of the first node; from there the whole sequence is recoverable by following links until you hit Λ. The nodes may be scattered anywhere in memory, in any physical order. The logical order lives entirely in the thread.

This trades away one thing and buys another. You lose random access: to reach the fifth item you must walk the first four — there is no arithmetic shortcut, because the successor is stored, not computed. What you gain is that insertion and deletion in the middle cost a single pointer write instead of shifting an entire array. The instrument below is a heap of scattered memory cells. Tie the thread from HEAD through them and make a list; then cut a node out and watch the thread heal.

Every cell holds a value and one link — the address of the next cell. The order you read is the order of the thread, not of the addresses. Cut a node and only one pointer changes: pred.link ← node.link. The list is healed in O(1); the freed cell rejoins the AVAIL pool.

II · The Healing CutDeletion as a single pointer write

Here is the move that made linked structures worth their cost. To delete a node X from the middle of an array you must shift every later element down one slot — O(n) work, touching memory you had no interest in. To delete X from a linked list you find its predecessor P and perform exactly one assignment: P.link ← X.link. The thread that used to run P → X → X.next now runs straight from P to X.next. Nothing else in memory moves. In the instrument you can watch the pointer's endpoint slide off the doomed node and reattach to its successor: the list heals over the wound.

One assignment closes the gap. The deleted cell is not destroyed — it is handed back to the AVAIL list, the pool of free cells waiting to be used again.

That freed cell is the seed of a second idea. Knuth's storage discipline keeps all unused nodes on their own linked list — the AVAIL list. Allocation is "pop a node off AVAIL"; freeing is "push it back on." Both are O(1) pointer writes. Press ＋ Alloc and a cell is drawn from the free pool and spliced into the thread; cut a node and it returns. But what happens when a program forgets to hand cells back — when the thread is dropped while nodes still float in memory, unreachable and unrecoverable? That question opens the next room, The Specter of Forgetting, and the invention of garbage collection.

III · When the Thread BreaksThe perils of the pointer

The pointer's freedom is also its hazard. Follow a link to a node that has already been freed and you have a dangling pointer — Theseus tugging a thread that leads to a chamber that no longer exists. Let two links point into the same node and a deletion can sever a piece of the list you meant to keep. Let a node's link, directly or through a chain, point back to an earlier node and you have a cycle: a traversal that never reaches Λ and never stops. The instrument's traversal guards against the last of these — it watches for a chamber it has already visited — because an algorithm that walks a linked structure must always ask not only "where does the thread lead?" but "have I been here before?"

"An algorithm must be seen to be believed." — D. E. Knuth

Newell, Shaw, and Simon reached for lists because human reasoning is associative — one thought leads to the next — and rigid arrays could not model it. Seventy years later the linked node is everywhere the shape of a computation is not known in advance: the free lists inside every memory allocator, the hash-table chains of Exhibit 3.3, the adjacency lists of every graph, the parse trees of every compiler. All of it descends from one small decision — to store, next to each datum, the address of what comes next — and from a girl at the mouth of a labyrinth, handing over a spool of thread.
